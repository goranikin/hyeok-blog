I've been using Omnifocus 4 (OFO) for schedule management since September 2025, when I felt I needed to manage my to-dos more systemically
while working at Dalpha. Over time, I've gradually developed my own way of using it. Here's what I've learned.

1. Brief thoughts

I believe no tool can magically manage your schedule for you. We should focus on building internal habits, not on any specific methodology.
With that in mind, I'd like to emphasize the positive feedback loop between using a to-do app and developing a sense of self-efficacy in
self-management; so that you can continously improve how you manage your day and build a solid habit. Of course, I don't mean that
to-do apps are useless, but rather that the combination of both is what matters. So, here's how I currently handle my to-dos!

2. Perspectives

Here are the perspectives I use:
- Inbox: A place to capture any thought as-is, without refinement. The key is to get ideas down as quickly as possible.
- Project: Long-term series of actions grouped into projects. All actions from the Inbox eventually get moved here.
- Forecast: The OFO's built-in view, showing all actions organized by data.
- Daily Execution: Shows actions that are flagged or due today. 
- Deep Work: Shows actions that requires significant effort or focus. Reviewing this view helps me prepare for demanding tasks in advance.
- No Due Date: Shows actions without a due date, preventing them from silently dying in my system.

3. Project Categories

A project is a goal that requires multiple actions to accomplish. For example, if I'm preparing for the TOEFL, I can set up a series of
actions like "Practice writing on a random topic once a week," etc. After organizing all those actions under the project "Get a High
TOEFL Score," I follow them in order. Once I complete everything, I remove the project. However, some projects are permanent and recurring
rather than one-shot -- like "Home Maintenance." Some actions, of course, exist on their own. There are stored in the "Quick Tasks" project
as one-shot tasks.

Each project can belong to a higher grouping called a "folder." Here are my folders.
- Active Projects: Contains active, in-process projects. Once a project achieves its goal, it is removed.
- Areas of Responsibility: Contains 5 permanent projects that I should handle in my entire life.
- Someday & Maybe: Contains undetermined or canceled projects, being still somewhat valuable to store their information.
- Waiting For: Projects waiting on external input -- nothing to do until I hear back.

There are five categories in the "Areas of Responsibility" folder.
- Career Development: Actions related to my personal development. Recently, most of them are kind of studying English.
- Relationship: Actions related to any relationships like "call or send a text message to someone".
- Health & Fitness: Workout plan for a week. Considering my personal schedule and set up its sort and duration.
- Finanlcial Management: Financial plans or habits.
- Home Maintanance: Household chores. I just follow actions rather memorize like when I should replace a toothbrush or a razor blade.

that can be memorized and executed with minimal conscious effort, and eventually done automatically.
